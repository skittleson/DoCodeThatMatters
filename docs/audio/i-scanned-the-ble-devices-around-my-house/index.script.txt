I scanned the BLE devices around my house - they are sharing everything

Many devices, such as a TV, only broadcast Bluetooth low energy while they are on, then disappear when they turn off. That means anyone standing outside your house with a laptop can tell when you're watching TV, when you get up, and when you go to bed. No pairing, no password, nothing to hack.

I built a tool to explore exactly this: bluetooth-wos, a terminal scanner inspired by DEFCON's Wall of Sheep that sort of fingerprints nearby BLE devices past their randomized MACs, estimates their distance from signal strength, and uses known devices to calibrate the rest. You can find the link to the tool on the blog post. This post walks through how it works and how to stop your own devices from leaking this stuff.

TL;DR: BLE devices leak more than you'd think. I built a tool that can scan and fingerprint devices past their randomized MACs, estimate distance from signal strength, and use one known device to calibrate the rest.

I've seen enough security-based issues to understand this could be a problem. Lately, I've been interested in when devices come and go. First, visitors around me. Second, how many people are in this area. Third, for security reasons—should this device be here? Fourth, why is this device no longer present?

How I scan: explore the devices around you now

From your phone, use nRF Connect. It is a great app that easily scans devices around you and allows looking through service and characteristics data, such as temperature, humidity, and battery levels. You can find the link to the app on the blog post.

Now, try it with a Python library called bleak. It is a common Python library that can be imported to do a quick basic scan. You would install it with the command: pip install bleak.

Copy and paste this into your Python REPL.

First, we import the necessary modules: asyncio and BleakScanner.

Next, we define a function called detection_callback. This function takes a device and advertising data as inputs. It gets the device name, or sets it to 'None' if it's missing. It retrieves the RSSI, which is the signal strength, from the advertising data, or sets it to None if not present. It also looks for transmission power, checking both lowercase 'tx_power' and capitalized 'TxPower' attributes, setting it to None if neither is found. It then creates a list with the device address and name. If the RSSI is not None, it appends the RSSI value to the list. If the transmission power is not None, it appends that value. Finally, it joins these parts with spaces and prints them.

Then, we define an async function called main. It creates a BleakScanner using our detection_callback. It starts the scanner, waits for ten seconds, and then stops it.

Finally, we run the main function.

Beating MAC randomization

Random MAC addresses help, but they will not fully protect you. A MAC changing every few minutes doesn't mean you can't be tracked. We still see some pretty interesting information, such as manufacturer data, and there are ways to overcome the rotation. For example, the name of the device or the RSSI value staying the same for long periods of time lets you re-link a "new" MAC to the same physical device.

Company or Manufacturer of each device

There is a public source of company identifiers which can be helpful to identify common devices. It makes it easier to find exploits and or features. You can find the link to this source on the blog post.

Services

These devices can have well-known Bluetooth GATT services or characteristics. Temperature, humidity, and battery service are common. Auto-resolving these can be tricky since most require a device to "notify".

Ranging: how far away is a device?

The image depicts device distances on a cartesian plane.

The official way to range is to use the device transmission power, receiving power, and signal propagation. This value changes in a line of sight, through walls, or with heavy interference. You can find a quick reference and a research paper on the blog post.

The formula for distance is: ten to the power of (transmission power minus RSSI) divided by (ten times the signal propagation constant).

The signal propagation constant is based on the environment, like walls, interference, metal, and really anything that can stop a signal. The range is usually between two and ten, with two being in an open space and ten with heavy interference. Typical modern drywall homes tend to see n equals four to six for straight-line-of-sight paths. I use eight in a modern home—that's a conservative value, better suited for heavily obstructed multi-wall paths.

How do you do it with a device's RSSI only? Not nearly as accurate, but there is an approximation.

The formula is: RSSI equals C minus ten times n times the log base ten of the distance.

Calibrating unknown devices against known ones

This is a practical part of this post and why I think it's the most useful: finding device distance with a known constant device. The tool collects all devices that do have a known TX power, builds a log-linear regression model of log base ten of distance versus RSSI, then uses that model to estimate distances for the devices that don't. The reference TX power is derived from the known device rather than assuming some common default value.

A quick illustrative example. Say one device advertises its TX power and the model puts it at roughly five meters at an RSSI of minus 60. Another nearby device with no TX power is coming in at about minus 62. Since it sits on almost the same point of the fitted curve, the model estimates it at a similar range: call it five to six meters. Drop to an RSSI of minus 75 and the same model pushes the estimate out toward twelve to fifteen meters. One calibrated anchor, and suddenly every anonymous device on the scan has a rough position. These are rough estimates! Not precise ranging since RSSI is noisy. Every device with an asterisk next to it is ranged.

Presence detection: who's home, and when

Static BLE Devices equal Surveillance Tools

Static devices that never ever move: TVs, lights, temps, smart locks, etc. The interesting thing about these is they are location indicators and or human presence indicators. For example, many TVs with BLE don't show up until the TV is on and disappear when it's off. This could give insight into whether someone is watching TV (or simply that a TV is on). Which means it also leaks when a house goes quiet and everyone heads to bed. I assume this is a way to save power or turn off the functionality when it's not needed.

Static devices that do move... like AirTags, Tiles, and BLE tags are really interesting since this allows tracking of coming and going. Those MACs don't change often (usually fifteen minutes) since they are meant to be found. You can see this feature is now showing up as a problem since Apple and Google now notify you when a device you don't know is following you.

Put all these together in a persistent scan, and it turns into a presence log:

First, human presence. A TV being on or off is a clear indicator of whether someone is home or going to bed. An AirTag or Tile seen, then disappearing, then reappearing at certain times of the day indicates when someone is coming home.

Second, the ability to get distances using other devices that have transmission power.

Third, exposure of semi-sensitive information via device metadata.

Fourth, how many individuals are within the area.

Can you detect someone scanning for devices?

It depends on how they're scanning. Passive scanning, like listening, is invisible. The radio is just receiving, so there is not much to detect. Active scanning is possible to detect since a scanner will send SCAN_REQ to advertisers. This is very low level, so you're not going to be able to capture it with a phone or laptop easily. Verdict here: nearly impossible to detect.

How to protect yourself

This section matters as much as the scanning. Reduce your exposure with the following concrete steps:

First, disable Bluetooth on devices that don't need it. TVs are the big one—if you're not using BLE remotes or casting, turn it off in the TV's settings. Same for smart plugs, bulbs, and anything that broadcasts 24/7.

Second, use Airplane Mode in sensitive areas. It stops your phone (and its accessories) from advertising altogether. Toggle it when you don't want to be trackable.

Third, turn on unknown-tracker alerts. Both Apple and Google now warn you when an unfamiliar AirTag or Tile-style tracker is following you.
For iOS, alerts are on by default; you can also run Settings, Privacy & Security, Safety Check.
For Android, go to Settings, Safety & emergency, Unknown tracker alerts, and the standalone "Find My Device" scanning.

Fourth, audit what's broadcasting. Run a scanner—nRF Connect, or the tool above—around your own home to find surprising devices around you and lock them down.
