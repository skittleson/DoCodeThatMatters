Parametric Belt Driven 3D Printed Spur Gear for Electric Skateboards

Use at your own risk. It's been a few years riding on this 3D printed spur gear. Let's give an update from this Reddit post I did a few years ago. You can find the link to that post on the blog post.

Specs

You can download the FreeCAD version on GitHub by following the link on the blog post.

First, it was printed with ABS at 0.3 millimeter resolution. Second, it uses a 265 dash 5M dash 9 5M Timing Belt, which measures 265 millimeters outside circumference, 9 millimeters width, and 5 millimeters pitch. Third, graphite powder on the belt, such as pencil lead, is used to smooth out any noise. Fourth, the design is fully parametric regarding gear teeth, mounting holes, inner cut out, and height. Fifth, it is used with an 80 millimeter wheel. Sixth, it is used with a BLDC motor at 8 Newton meters of force, 70 amps peak with a 12s battery. Seventh, the cost to print is one dollar and 45 cents. Eighth, the top speed with this existing configuration is 25 miles per hour. And finally, the rider weighs 200 pounds.

Wear and Tear

This was about 100 plus hours of riding. It's hard to take a picture with it mounted. Here is a side view until I take it off the truck. A better image is coming soon.

Here is a view of the inner gear side with the belt, showing the condition of the teeth and the mounting area after extensive use.

First, the gear teeth have worn down as expected. Second, it was noticeably louder than a metal gear due to the pattern chosen for the teeth. Adding graphite powder every 10 plus rides keeps it sounding smooth and prevents wear. Third, the nuts came loose a few times, so use lock tight. Fourth, belt wear was nominal. I expect to change it every 500 plus hours of riding. Fifth, the bottom land could be more round to fit the belt better. Sixth, there are no noticeable cracks or layer splitting in the 3D print. Seventh, the amount of material the bolt and nut can hold onto, or lack thereof, is a consideration. It could "rip off" the end of the plastic gear. Using longer bolts then removing the inner "cut out cone" could help with that, as well as using an additional nut for surface area tension. There are no concerns as of yet since the wearing has been nominal. Eighth, use double wall bearings. Even a little water and dirt can cause the bearings to crack at higher speeds. That was a hard lesson.

Next Possible Experiment

Helical gears or double helical gears with no belt seem like an experiment to try out. It offers a lot of benefits such as no belt, no belt slipping, quieter operation, and only 3D printed parts.
