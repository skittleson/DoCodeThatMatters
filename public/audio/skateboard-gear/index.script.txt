You know how I've been riding my DIY electric skateboard for years now? Well, it's time for an update on the gear situation.

A few years ago, I posted about using a parametric 3D printed spur gear on Reddit. You can find the link to that post on the blog for this story. It's been a while since then, and I've put in over 100 hours of riding on this gear.

So, let me walk you through some specs. The gear was printed with ABS at a resolution of 0.3 millimeters. I used a 265-5M-9 timing belt, which has an outside circumference of 265 millimeters and a width of 9 millimeters, with a pitch of 5 millimeters. To reduce noise, I applied graphite powder to the belt, similar to using pencil lead.

Now, here's what's really cool about this gear: it's fully parametric. That means you can adjust the teeth, mounting holes, inner cut out, and height to your heart's content. And with the FreeCAD design available for download on the blog, you can play around with it yourself.

You can see a 3D rendering of the model in action if you check out the blog post. It shows the parametric adjustments I made to get it just right for my 190 kilovolt BLDC motor with 16 tooth gear. And don't worry about safety – this motor is plenty powerful, with 8 newton-meters of force and a peak current of 70 amps on a 12-s battery.

As for the actual riding experience, I've clocked speeds up to 25 miles per hour with my existing configuration. And that's not bad considering the rider's weight is around 200 pounds.

Now, let's talk about wear and tear. After all those hours of riding, you'd expect some damage, right? Well, the gear teeth did wear down as expected, but I've been able to smooth out the noise by adding graphite powder every now and then. The belt itself has worn nominal – I expect to change it in around 500 hours.

There are a few things that need attention, though. For one, the nuts on the bolts tend to come loose sometimes, so be sure to use lock tight. And while there's no notable cracking or layer splitting in the print, I do notice that the bottom land could be more rounded to fit the belt better. Not a big deal, but something to keep in mind.

Oh, and one last thing: double-wall bearings are essential for this setup. Water and dirt can cause them to crack at higher speeds, so don't skimp on those.

Finally, I'm considering experimenting with helical gears or double helical gears next. It's an interesting idea – no belt would mean less maintenance and fewer slipping issues. And it looks like a quieter ride might be in my future too.

You can find more details about the design and experiments on the blog post itself.
