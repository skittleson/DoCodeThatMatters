The biggest rule for home automation is not to change existing behavior of users that causes inconvenience or frustration. You know, like when you're used to flipping a switch to turn on and off a light in your living room. But then suddenly you have to ask Alexa, Google, or use an app on your phone. That's just bad.

The user can't just flip a switch like they're used to. Now, let's look at some solutions presented in this situation.

There are two common solutions. The first one is blocking the user with tape or other mechanisms. You can find examples of this on websites that specialize in 3D printing designs. But this changes user behavior, and it's not a good idea.

The second solution is replacing the light switch with a smarter one. This is the best option, but it requires a hardware replacement of the light switch, which may not be possible, costly, or even dangerous to do-it-yourself!

Now, here's a third hybrid solution: trigger the smart device with the existing light switch using power wall outlet as feedback, using an Arduino device with WiFi. The light switch changes the power state of the outlet that the Arduino-based microcontroller is monitoring. When the light switch is turned on or off, it changes the power to the Arduino analog voltage GPIO pin, which sends a message off to a home assistance to notify the smart device.

### Step 1: Get the Components and Solder Them

You'll need a few tools for this step. You'll need a soldering iron and either a third hand or vise. For the materials, you can use a Wemos mini d1 from Amazon or similar esp8266/esp32 device, two x resistors (any two usually work), wire, two 5v USB wall chargers, one male USB plugin, one regular USB charging cable for the esp32, an LED, solder, and optionally, a BME280 or BME680.
