If you're anything like me, you've probably got an old Duplicator i3 3D printer sitting in your garage or on a shelf somewhere. You might be wondering what to do with it, since it's been surpassed by newer models.

Well, I took it upon myself to upgrade mine instead of just getting rid of it. And let me tell you, it was quite the journey! In this post, I'll walk you through all the steps I took to modernize my Duplicator i3 and turn it into a capable 3D printer again.

So, let's take a look at the list of things I wanted to upgrade or replace on my Duplicator i3:

First up, we've got the glass bed. Replacing this was one of the first steps I took, and it made a big difference right off the bat.

Next, I decided to upgrade the steppers with 1/16 to 1/32 steps. This provides much cleaner and smoother prints than before.

Now, let's talk about the electronics. As you can see from my image of the MKS Gen 1.4 board wired up, this is a major upgrade over the old Melzi board that came with the printer. And if you're interested in knowing more about the differences between these two boards, I've got a link to some documentation on my blog.

One thing I had to figure out was how to connect all the end stops properly. If you look at the image of the 3 pin JST connector, you'll see that it's not as straightforward as it seems. Luckily, there are adapters available that make this process much easier.

Moving on to the firmware, I had to make some changes to get everything working smoothly. Specifically, I had to invert the end stops and double the step count for the DRV8825 drivers.

I also experimented with replacing the display, but that was a bit more tricky than expected. The connectors are reversed on the RepRap Discount Full Smart Controller, which made things a bit of a puzzle to solve.

Power supply-wise, I upgraded from a 20A to a 30A unit. This is an important upgrade, as it helps prevent overheating issues that can be common with these printers.

Next up was the Y bracket, which got replaced with something sturdier and more adjustable.

Other upgrades included adding an easy access case for the MKS Gen board, installing a filament sensor, and covering the power supply for safety reasons.
